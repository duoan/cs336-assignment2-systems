\paragraph{(c) Effect of removing warm-up steps.}

We re-run the large model (969M params) with warmup $\in \{0, 1, 5\}$
to isolate the effect of warm-up on measured timings (Table~\ref{tab:warmup-effect}).

Without warm-up (warmup=0), the first iteration is dramatically slower:
the forward pass takes 295ms compared to $\sim$62ms in steady state (a $\sim$4.8$\times$ slowdown),
and the backward pass reaches 286ms vs.\ $\sim$224ms.
The total for iteration 0 is 629ms---1.6$\times$ the steady-state $\sim$384ms.
This is because CUDA performs lazy initialization on the first kernel launch, including
context creation, kernel JIT compilation, and memory pool allocation.

With warmup=1, the first measured iteration still shows overhead
(73ms forward vs.\ $\sim$60ms steady state),
because a single warm-up step only partially amortizes cuDNN autotuning and
memory allocator warm-up.
By warmup=5 (our default), the overhead on iteration 0 shrinks to
69ms forward---only $\sim$16\% above steady state---and
iterations 1--9 are fully stable at $\sim$381ms total.

The steady-state timings (iterations 4--9) are consistent across all warm-up settings
at $\sim$381--385ms total, confirming that warm-up only affects early measurements and
5 warm-up steps are sufficient for reliable benchmarking.

\subsection{Nsight Systems Profiling}
\textbf{Problem (nsys\_profile)}

We profiled two model sizes---\textbf{small} (128.6M params) and
\textbf{large} (969.4M params)---at three power-of-two context lengths each,
using \texttt{nsys profile} with 5 warmup + 5 measurement iterations.
The small model was profiled at context lengths 256, 1024, and 2048 (the
maximum that fits in GPU memory); the large model at 256, 512, and 1024.

\paragraph{(a) Total time on forward pass.}

Table~\ref{tab:nsys-forward} shows the GPU-projected forward pass time
per iteration from the \texttt{nvtx\_gpu\_proj\_sum} report.
The GPU-projected time measures the actual GPU execution span of kernels
launched within the \texttt{forward\_pass} NVTX range, and is generally
\emph{higher} than the Python \texttt{timeit} measurement (which only
records CPU dispatch time without \texttt{torch.cuda.synchronize()}).
For example, for the large model at context length 512, nsys reports a
GPU forward time of 111.2\,ms, while \texttt{timeit} records
$\sim$60.6\,ms---reflecting only how long the CPU takes to dispatch
forward kernels asynchronously.
The wall-clock total per iteration ($\sim$382\,ms with synchronization)
is consistent with the sum of GPU forward, backward, and optimizer times.

\begin{table}[H]

\caption{GPU-projected forward pass time per iteration (ms).}
\label{tab:nsys-forward}
\begin{tabular}{llrr}
  \toprule
  Model & Context & GPU Forward (ms) & Python \texttt{timeit} Forward (ms) \\
  \midrule
  small & 256  &  11.9 & --- \\
  small & 1024 &  50.9 & --- \\
  small & 2048 & 145.3 & --- \\
  \midrule

The forward pass time grows roughly quadratically with context length
(e.g., small: $11.9 \to 50.9 \to 145.3$\,ms for $256 \to 1024 \to 2048$),
consistent with the $O(T^2)$ attention scaling dominating at longer sequences.

\paragraph{(b) Most time-consuming CUDA kernel.}

The most time-consuming CUDA kernel across all profiles is the
CUTLASS SGEMM kernel (single-precision general matrix multiply),
which implements all matmul operations in the Transformer (linear
projections, attention score computation, FFN layers).
Table~\ref{tab:nsys-top-kernels} shows the top kernel for each configuration.

and reduction operations.

\paragraph{(c) Non-matmul kernels with non-trivial runtime.}

Table~\ref{tab:nsys-gemm-frac} shows that GEMM kernels account for
49--61\% of total GPU kernel time during a full training step.
The remaining time is spent on several non-matmul kernel categories:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]

    implementing LayerNorm scaling, residual additions, and activation functions.
  \item \texttt{elementwise\_kernel} (div, subtract): 3--7\%,
    used in softmax normalization and gradient computation.
  \item \texttt{reduce\_kernel}: $\sim$3\%, performing reductions
    for LayerNorm mean/variance and loss computation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(d) Full training step profile comparison.}

Comparing the GEMM fraction across configurations
(Table~\ref{tab:nsys-gemm-frac}), we observe two trends:
(1)~the GEMM fraction decreases with increasing context length
(e.g., small: $58.8\% \to 43.4\% \to 32.1\%$ for
$256 \to 1024 \to 2048$), because attention-related elementwise
operations (softmax, masking, scaling) grow as $O(T^2)$ while
FFN matmuls grow only as $O(T)$; and
(2)~during a full training step (forward + backward + optimizer),
the GEMM fraction is lower than inference-only would suggest because
the backward pass adds gradient elementwise operations and the
AdamW optimizer performs per-parameter elementwise updates
(momentum, variance, weight decay) that are entirely non-GEMM.

\paragraph{(e) Softmax vs.\ matrix multiplication runtime comparison.}

Table~\ref{tab:nsys-attn-breakdown} shows the GPU-projected time per call
(median) for the three annotated sub-operations within
\texttt{scaled\_dot\_product\_attention}: the $QK^T$ matmul
(\texttt{computing attention scores}), softmax, and the
$\text{attn} \times V$ matmul (\texttt{final matmul}).

  \midrule

Despite having $\sim$25$\times$ fewer FLOPs than the two attention
matmuls combined ($O(T^2)$ for softmax vs.\ $O(T^2 \cdot d_{\text{head}})$
for each matmul, where $d_{\text{head}} = 64$), softmax takes
\emph{comparable or even more} wall-clock time.
This is because softmax is \textbf{memory-bound}: it must read and write
the entire $[B, H, T, T]$ attention score matrix multiple times
(for row-max subtraction, exponentiation, row-sum, and division),
while the matmul kernels are \textbf{compute-bound} and can effectively
utilize the GPU's SIMT units at high arithmetic intensity.
As context length increases, the softmax-to-matmul runtime ratio grows
(from $\sim$0.9$\times$ to $\sim$1.8$\times$), because the
memory-bound softmax becomes increasingly dominant over the
compute-bound matmuls at larger attention matrices.

\subsubsection{Benchmarking Mixed Precision}
\textbf{Problem (benchmarking\_mixed\_precision)}

\paragraph{(a) Data types under FP16 autocasting.}

\begin{itemize}
  \item the model parameters within the autocast context?: \textbf{FP32}
  \item the output of the first feed-forward layer? \textbf{FP16}
  \item the output of layer norm? \textbf{FP32}, since layer norm has reduce operation (mean)
  \item the model's predicted logits? \textbf{FP16}, from the last fc2, which will use FB16
  \item the loss? \textbf{FP32}
  \item the model's gradients? \textbf{FP32}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(b) Layer normalization and BF16.}

For FP16 mixed precision, layer norm's mean/variance computation and the scaling by
$\beta$ and $\alpha$ are numerically sensitive, because they involve reductions and divisions 
that can loss precision and underflow in FP16.

BF16 mixed precision yields a $1.71\times$--$1.93\times$ end-to-end speedup.
The per-phase breakdown (with \texttt{torch.cuda.synchronize()} barriers
between phases) reveals a clear pattern:
the \textbf{forward pass} benefits most ($2.0$--$3.2\times$), with speedup
growing as model size increases and GEMMs become more compute-bound;
the \textbf{backward pass} also accelerates ($1.8$--$2.4\times$) because
autograd reuses the BF16 activations saved during the forward pass;
the \textbf{optimizer step} is essentially unchanged ($\approx1.0\times$)
because AdamW operates on FP32 master weights regardless of autocast.
The forward-pass speedup exceeds $2\times$ even for the smallest model,
confirming that BF16 Tensor Cores provide genuine throughput gains rather
than just memory savings.

  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{benchmark_results/memory/xl_ctx128_train.png}
  \caption{Full training step. The forward pass builds up saved-for-backward
    activations (rising slope), the backward pass frees them while
    allocating gradients (peak then decline), and the optimizer step
    keeps memory flat.}
  \label{fig:mem-train}
\end{subfigure}
\caption{Active memory timeline for the xl model (ctx=128) from
  PyTorch \texttt{memory\_viz}.}\label{fig:mem-timelines}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Inference-only (forward pass):} The timeline shows a single
``staircase'' pattern --- memory climbs layer-by-layer as each
\texttt{TransformerBlock} computes its output, then stays flat after
the final layer.  No gradients or optimizer states are allocated, so
the peak ($\approx$12.9 GiB) sits only slightly above the
model-weight baseline ($\approx$12.7 GiB).  Intermediate tensors are
allocated and freed within each layer, producing small
sawtooth spikes on top of the staircase.

\textbf{Full training step (forward + backward + optimizer):}  The
timeline has three distinct phases:
(1) the forward pass creates the same staircase, but now all
activations saved for autograd (``residuals'') remain allocated,
pushing memory steadily upward;
(2) the backward pass then frees those residuals layer-by-layer
\emph{while} allocating gradient tensors, producing a characteristic
``mountain'' that peaks at the transition point ($\approx$51.4 GiB
overall, since optimizer states are already present from warmup);
(3) the optimizer step performs in-place updates, so memory stays
roughly flat.
The three phases are clearly distinguishable from the shape of the
timeline.

At context length 128 the forward-only peak is dominated by the

The effect of BF16 mixed precision on memory depends on the
balance between two opposing factors:
\texttt{torch.autocast} keeps model parameters in FP32 and lazily
caches BF16 copies of each weight matrix ($\approx$6.3 GiB overhead),
but activations and saved-for-backward tensors are stored in BF16
(half the FP32 size).
For \textbf{inference at short context} (128), the weight-cache
overhead dominates and BF16 uses \emph{more} memory ($+$6.2 GiB).
For \textbf{training at short context}, the two effects nearly cancel:
51.42 vs.\ 51.41 GiB.
For \textbf{training at long context} (2048, batch 1), the larger
activation volume tips the balance and BF16 \emph{saves} 8.7 GiB
(91.16 $\to$ 82.49 GiB).
In summary, mixed precision does not significantly reduce memory for
short-context workloads but provides meaningful savings when
activation memory dominates (long context, large batch).

\paragraph{(d) Size of activation tensor in residual stream.}

For the xl model with batch size $B{=}4$, context length $T{=}512$,
and $d_\text{model}{=}2560$, the residual-stream activation tensor
has shape $(B, T, d_\text{model}) = (4, 512, 2560)$.  In FP32,
each element is 4 bytes, so the tensor size is:
\[
  4 \times 512 \times 2560 \times 4 \;\text{bytes}
  = 20\,971\,520 \;\text{bytes}
  = \frac{20\,971\,520}{1024^2} = 20 \;\text{MiB}.
\]

\paragraph{(e) Largest allocations in memory timeline.}

When reducing the ``Detail'' level in \texttt{pytorch.org/memory\_viz},
the two dominant allocation sizes that remain visible are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{100 MiB} -- FFN weight matrices.  Each has shape
    $d_\text{model} \times d_\text{ff} = 2560 \times 10240$ in FP32:
    $2560 \times 10240 \times 4 / 1024^2 = 100$ MiB.
    There are 96 such blocks (32 layers $\times$ 3 FFN weights per
    layer: gate, up, and down projections), totalling 9.38 GiB.
  \item \textbf{$\sim$25 MiB} -- Attention projection weight matrices.
    Each has shape $d_\text{model} \times d_\text{model} = 2560 \times
    2560$ in FP32: $2560 \times 2560 \times 4 / 1024^2 = 25$ MiB
    (allocated as 26 MiB by the CUDA caching allocator).
    There are 128 such blocks (32 layers $\times$ 4 projections:
    $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O$), totalling 3.25 GiB.
\end{itemize}

Together these model-parameter allocations account for
$9.38 + 3.25 = 12.63$ GiB, consistent with the $\sim$12.7 GiB baseline
observed in the memory timeline.  The stack traces confirm they come
from \texttt{torch.nn.Parameter} initialization inside
\texttt{TransformerBlock} linear layers.

\paragraph{(f) Memory saved for backward per TransformerBlock.}

Using \texttt{nsys profile --cuda-memory-usage=true -t cuda,nvtx} combined
with per-block NVTX ranges and \texttt{torch.cuda.memory\_allocated()} hooks,
we measured memory allocated during a single \texttt{TransformerBlock}
forward pass on the xl model (batch 4, ctx 128, FP32).  Each block
saves $\sim$\textbf{166 MiB} of activations for the backward pass.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Per-operation breakdown of saved-for-backward memory
  within one \texttt{TransformerBlock} (xl, ctx=128, FP32).}
\label{tab:saved-for-bw}
\begin{tabular}{l r r}
\toprule
Operation & $\Delta$ Memory (MiB) & \% of Total \\
\midrule
FFN (SwiGLU)       & 105.0 & 63.2\% \\
Attention (MHSA)   &  41.2 & 24.8\% \\
RMSNorm (ln1)      &  10.0 &  6.0\% \\
RMSNorm (ln2)      &  10.0 &  6.0\% \\
Residual add (net) &   0.0 &  0.0\% \\
\midrule
Total per block    & 166.2 & 100\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

SwiGLU dominates because its three linear projections ($w_1, w_2, w_3$)
each save their input tensors for \texttt{backward}, and the element-wise
\texttt{silu} and multiplication further save intermediate activations
(five $d_\text{ff}$-sized tensors of 20 MiB each $+$ one
$d_\text{model}$-sized input of 5 MiB $= 105$ MiB).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Backward-pass gradient memory.}
During backward through each block, the saved-for-backward tensors
($\sim$166 MiB) are freed, but new gradient tensors are created.
We observed:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Forward delta (saved for backward): $+166.3$ MiB per block.
  \item Backward net memory change: $+238.7$ MiB per block.
  \item Gradient memory created = freed $+$ net change
    $= 166.3 + 238.7 = 405.0$ MiB.
\end{itemize}
Each \texttt{TransformerBlock} has 104,862,720 weight parameters, so
the expected gradient memory is $104{,}862{,}720 \times 4 \;\text{bytes}
= 400.0$ MiB.  The observed 405 MiB is within 1.3\% of the expected
value; the small overhead comes from temporary intermediate gradient
tensors allocated during backpropagation through composite operations.
This confirms the result matches expectation.
